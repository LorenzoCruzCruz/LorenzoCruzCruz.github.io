Computer Aided Design

I began the design in Autodesk’s Fusion 360, starting with the Clinostat component on the top left of the screen.

Open a new component and name it “Base”.

Create a square sketch with the origin at the center. Dimensions are crude, but for the sake of an example, it should be labeled at 0.5 inches.

Select the sketch, and extrude it by 0.5. To save some steps, create 2 separate components named the “Rotational Base” and another named “Circular Rotational Base”. These will be the components where you will create the next parts of the Clinostat model.

Select the surface of the base, and create a sketch on the upper right corner and stretch it downwards to create a rectangle.

Extrude the wall to 0.4 inches tall.

Click the newly created wall and select the circle tool, and place the circle in the upper middle quadrant at 0.1 inches.

Extrude the circle on both sides of the wall (the join function). The size doesn’t necessarily matter, the circle on the inner part of the base will be covered by a rectangle, and the outside circle will act as a spinning component.

Click the Rotational Base component and create a sketch on the inside circle and create a rectangle, this rectangle will be the base of the spinning circle where a plant will be held.

Extrude the rectangle to be the same side as the lower base level, and do the joint function to pull the rectangle towards the wall a bit more.

Click the Circular Rotational Base component, and select the roof of the newly made rectangle. Create a small circle that will act as the stand for the bigger rotational circle.

Extrude that circle to be about 0.25 inches high.

Next, select the roof of that circle and create a new circle sketch with a diameter of 0.40.

Select the sketch, and extrude it upwards by 0.7 inches. Ignore the hole in the middle for now, it will be addressed in the following steps.

Open a sketch on the roof of the tall circle and make it 0.35 inches in diameter. We will cut this downward to create the compartment that a plant will be held.

Use that sketch to cut into the model 0.68 inches downwards, successfully making the space that will hold the plant.

These are the schematics and measurements of what I could capture from the sketch; be mindful that this is rough 3D draft. Measurements are subject to change at any point throughout the remainder of this project.

I’ll be using Affinity Design 2 for the creation of the logo draft for the company we’re using for the Growing Beyond Earth project.

Opening up to a blank canvas, I selected the rectangle tool and created it to a little less than half the side of the inner box.

Next, I selected the corner tool and grabbed the upper left corner till the circle within was the size of the rectangle to create a futuristic and simplistic design

Repeat step 3 with the bottom right hand corner.

Following the new rounded corners, use the gradient tool and place the 2 gradients on the opposite ends of the rectangle, the dots resting on the regular sharp edges—not the circular ones.

I’d decided to create a green-to-gray contrast, so this step was me messing around with different variations of green and gray till I found this set.

I wanted to incorporate the idea of botany and get a futuristic feel for it, so I tried to make the rectangle feel like a minimalistic leaf and incorporate the moon, which is why here you see me place a crescent moon within the rectangle.

Ultimately I didn’t enjoy the design, it wasn’t coming out the way I had envisioned it. I has scrapped the idea and began working on it with the idea of initials. I liked the industrial/space feel, so I thought of using a rhombus to create a sliced L and the crescent as a sort of J, to represent all my team members: Jack Sosa and Lorenzo De Toro.

I added a second rhombus towards the bottom and messed around with the edges to make sure the rhombus were separated, but close enough so that a person can make out that it’s supposed to be an L.

Add a crescent above the L, make it thinner than normal to represent an actual moon.

Add a second one, the reasoning I’ll explain in the next few steps.

Recolor the L to a bright blue to silver gradient, and change the moons to a bright orange to a yellowish green.

I picked the L because it represented the L in Lorenzo for one of my teammates and myself: Lorenzo Cruz-Cruz and Lorenzo De Toro. The crescent moons also can resemble a inverse and slanted J or S for Jack Sosa, my second teamate. Also, the crescent moons back to back make CC, or Cruz-Cruz, like my name.

Note: All of this can be subject to change, expect some updates and minor changes to the design! Every one of them will flow through here, so keep your eye out!
